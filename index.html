Intro

Welcome to my e-portfolio. This website introduces my academic background, interests, career goals, and engineering projects. I created this portfolio as part of my university e-portfolio assignment, but I also want it to serve as a professional record of my growth as an engineering student.

My academic interests are centered on semiconductor devices, microelectronics, and circuit technology. I am especially interested in understanding how device-level physics connects to real electrical behavior and how that knowledge can be applied to modern semiconductor systems.

About Me

I am currently studying Electrical Engineering at Georgia Tech. My interest in engineering grew from a deeper curiosity about how electronic systems work beyond their visible functions. Rather than seeing devices only as finished products, I became interested in the principles behind them, especially how voltage, current, materials, and structure interact to create real electronic behavior.

Over time, this curiosity developed into a stronger focus on semiconductor devices and circuits. I enjoy learning both the theoretical and practical sides of engineering, and I try to connect classroom concepts to projects and technical exploration.

This portfolio reflects both my technical interests and my long-term direction as an engineering student. My goal is to keep building the knowledge, projects, and communication skills needed for a strong career in the semiconductor industry.

Career Goals

My long-term goal is to become a semiconductor device engineer. I am especially interested in roles related to semiconductor devices, microelectronics, testing, and hardware-focused engineering.

In the short term, I want to strengthen my foundation in device physics, circuit analysis, and electronics through coursework and hands-on projects. I also want to continue building projects that demonstrate both technical depth and the ability to explain engineering work clearly.

In the long term, I hope to contribute to semiconductor technology through device-level and hardware-level work by connecting theory, measurement, and real-world application.
