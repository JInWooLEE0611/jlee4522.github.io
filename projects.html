MOSFET I-V Characteristic Analysis (ECE1100)

Ongoing

+

Spring 2026

Project Overview

This project focuses on understanding the fundamental physics behind MOSFET operation and experimentally examining its current–voltage (I–V) characteristics. To prepare for the experiment, I studied the theoretical foundations of semiconductor devices, including carrier behavior, energy-band concepts, p–n junction physics, and MOS capacitor behavior under different bias conditions.

Based on this theoretical background, I built a breadboard setup using an n-channel MOSFET to measure and analyze its I–V characteristics under different gate and drain bias conditions. The project emphasizes connecting device physics theory to practical measurement and data analysis.

My Experience / Project Concept

To prepare for this project, I focused on building a strong theoretical foundation in semiconductor physics. I studied concepts such as crystal lattice structure, energy–momentum (E–k) diagrams, density of states (DOS), Fermi level, and carrier behavior under thermal equilibrium and non-equilibrium conditions.

I also studied MOS capacitor physics and band diagram behavior under bias, with particular attention to depletion and inversion conditions. In addition, I explored threshold voltage formation, realistic threshold voltage behavior in practical devices, and current–voltage relationships in the linear and saturation regions of MOSFET operation.

Through this process, I connected theoretical device physics concepts to real experimental measurements by constructing a hardware setup, collecting I–V data, estimating threshold voltage, and analyzing device behavior using plotted measurement results.

Key Topics and Concepts Applied

  • MOS Capacitor Operation and Band Diagram Under Bias
  • Inversion Layer Formation and Threshold Voltage
  • Realistic Threshold Voltage Estimation from Measured Data
  • MOSFET I–V Characteristics (Linear and Saturation Regions)
  • Gate Leakage Characteristics (Ig–Vg and Ig–Vd Behavior)
  • Breadboard Circuit Implementation and Device Measurement
  • Experimental Data Collection and Curve Analysis

Skills

Digital Tram Control System Design(FSM)

Completed

−

Fall 2025

Project Overview

This project involved designing a digital tram control system for an airport transportation setting using a finite state machine (FSM). The client requirement was to create a control system based on four given signals, while the overall FSM structure and implementation strategy were left open-ended. I designed a 6-state tram control system that determines motion and stopping behavior based on the relative progression of states rather than absolute floor position, making the design applicable regardless of the number of stops.

A key objective of my design was hardware minimization. Starting from the state diagram and present-state / next-state relationships, I repeatedly refined the state table and applied Karnaugh map (K-map) minimization to exploit symmetry and repeated logic patterns. By maximizing useful groupings and reducing Boolean expressions, I implemented the system with only 3 D flip-flops, 5 AND gates, 3 OR gates, and 2 NOT gates. The project was verified through digital simulation and received a full score.

My Experience / Project Concept

This project helped me strengthen my understanding of sequential logic design beyond basic FSM construction. I learned how to move from a conceptual control problem to a complete hardware-oriented implementation by building a state diagram, deriving a state table, simplifying logic with K-maps, and validating behavior through simulation. More importantly, I focused on designing the control logic as efficiently as possible rather than only making it function, which gave me stronger insight into digital hardware optimization.

  • Sequential Logic Design
  • Karnaugh Map (K-map) Minimization
  • Boolean Logic Simplification

Band-Pass Filter Design & Analysis

Completed

−

Nov 2024 - Dec 2024

Project Overview

This project focused on the design, theoretical analysis, and experimental verification of a band-pass filter (BPF). The goal was to understand how the circuit passes signals within a specific frequency range while attenuating signals outside that range, and to compare theoretical predictions with measured experimental behavior.

I derived the transfer function in the Laplace domain, analyzed cutoff frequencies, center frequency, and bandwidth, and used Bode plot concepts to interpret the circuit response in the frequency domain. Experimental measurements were then collected and compared with theoretical calculations. The results showed that the measured bandwidth matched the theoretical value closely, while the center frequency showed a larger deviation that was ultimately explained by component tolerances in the resistors and capacitors.

My Experience / Project Concept

This project helped me strengthen my understanding of frequency-domain circuit analysis and the relationship between mathematical modeling and real experimental behavior. I learned how to move from circuit configuration to transfer-function derivation, estimate filter characteristics using theoretical expressions, and interpret discrepancies between theory and measurement. In particular, I gained practical insight into how component tolerances influence the center frequency and overall filter response in real hardware.
